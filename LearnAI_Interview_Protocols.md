# LearnAI Interview Protocols for ITiCSE / SIGCSE TS Resubmission

## Document Purpose
This document provides two complete interview protocols for the LearnAI Framework study:

1. `Protocol 1`: Layer 2 client interviews using stimulated recall
2. `Protocol 2`: tutor interviews focused on enactment of the 5-Stage Pedagogical Script

The protocols are designed to support a mixed-methods paper by linking qualitative accounts to the LearnAI Framework's core theoretical constructs:

- Two-layer intervention structure:
  - `Layer 1`: wide-exposure presentations
  - `Layer 2`: 1-on-1 co-creation sessions
- `5-Stage Pedagogical Script`:
  1. Problem Framing
  2. Tool-Task Mapping
  3. Iterative Co-Prompting
  4. Deployment & Verification
  5. Ethical Reflection
- Targeted cognitive shift:
  - from `AI as Oracle` (passive answer machine)
  - to `AI as Process Partner` (active collaborator in a human-led workflow)

---

# Shared Study Procedures

## Study Purpose Statement
The purpose of these interviews is to understand how participants experienced LearnAI Layer 2 co-creation sessions, how they made sense of AI before and after the session, what aspects of the process supported or hindered learning, and whether the LearnAI approach was enacted consistently enough to support replication.

## Recommended Qualitative Design
- `Client interviews`: semi-structured stimulated recall interviews anchored in each participant's deployed artifact
- `Tutor interviews`: semi-structured reflective interviews focused on implementation, adaptation, fidelity, and replicability
- `Analytic approach`: deductive-inductive thematic analysis
- `Integration`: connect themes to pre/post assessment scores, return behavior, client background, and artifact type

## IRB-Ready Core Language
Use the following language across both protocols, with local placeholders inserted as needed.

### Informed Consent Essentials
- Participation is voluntary.
- Participants may skip any question.
- Participants may stop the interview at any time without penalty.
- Participation or non-participation will not affect access to LearnAI services, grades, employment, standing at the university, or relationship with the research team.
- With permission, interviews will be audio-recorded for transcription and analysis.
- De-identified excerpts may be used in research publications or presentations.
- Names and direct identifiers will be removed from transcripts.
- Participants may request that the recording be stopped at any point.
- If a participant withdraws before data are de-identified and aggregated, their interview data will be deleted to the extent feasible.

### Confidentiality Language
"Your responses will be kept as confidential as possible. The interview will be assigned a study ID. Any recording or transcript will be stored on a secure, access-controlled university system. When we report findings, we will remove names and other direct identifiers. Because some artifacts or stories may contain distinctive details, complete anonymity cannot be guaranteed, but the research team will take reasonable steps to minimize identification."

## Recording and Transcription Instructions
- Use audio recording by default. Video recording is not necessary unless the IRB specifically approves it.
- For stimulated recall, display the artifact on screen, but record only audio unless screen recording has separate consent.
- Start recording only after verbal consent is obtained.
- State participant ID, date, interviewer initials, and interview type at the start.
- Upload recording the same day to a secure institutional folder.
- Transcribe with an approved secure transcription method.
- Review transcripts manually for:
  - names
  - course titles
  - departments
  - client names
  - URLs, app names, or organizational identifiers embedded in artifacts
- Replace identifiers with brackets, for example:
  - `[department]`
  - `[course]`
  - `[student name]`
- Maintain a separate encrypted linkage file mapping names to study IDs.
- Destroy raw recordings after transcription and verification if required by the approved protocol.

## Interviewer Training Recommendations
All interviewers should complete a short calibration session before data collection:
- Review the protocol together
- Practice timing
- Practice neutral probing
- Avoid leading participants toward "success" narratives
- Avoid over-explaining the 5-Stage Script during the interview
- Use the artifact as a recall anchor, not as proof of learning by itself
- Distinguish between participant memory and interviewer interpretation

## Data Linking Recommendations
For each interview, maintain a metadata sheet with:
- Study ID
- Interview date
- Interviewer
- Participant role: client or tutor
- Client subgroup or tutor subgroup
- Discipline/background
- Returner status
- Pre/post assessment scores if available
- Artifact type
- Session date
- Whether AI was continued independently
- Whether participant expressed resistance/rejection

# Protocol 1: Client Interviews (Stimulated Recall)

## Purpose
To understand how Layer 2 clients experienced the co-creation process, whether their mental model of AI shifted from `oracle` to `process partner`, what they learned or rejected, what surprised them, and whether they transferred the process independently after the session.

## Target Sample
- `8-10` Layer 2 clients
- `~20 minutes` each

## Purposive Sampling Guide
Aim for maximum variation with explicit boundary cases.

### Minimum Sampling Matrix
- `2-3` non-coders or low-technical participants
- `2` CS-adjacent participants
- `2` participants who returned for additional LearnAI support
- `1-2` participants who struggled, disengaged, or explicitly rejected AI
- Include disciplinary spread where possible:
  - nursing
  - CS
  - education
  - business
  - administrative staff
  - high school participants if approved and consent/assent procedures allow

### Sampling Notes
- If categories overlap, that is acceptable.
- Prioritize contrast over representativeness.
- Oversample negative or mixed cases if the first wave is too positive.
- If possible, include one participant whose artifact deployed successfully and one whose artifact stalled before deployment.

## Eligibility
Include clients who:
- completed at least one Layer 2 session
- can be contacted after the session
- consent to interview and recording
- can view and discuss their artifact

Exclude clients who:
- did not complete enough of a session to discuss process
- cannot be contacted securely
- are minors without approved assent/guardian consent procedures

## Client Stimulated Recall Setup

### Before the Interview
Prepare:
- participant study ID
- artifact, output, or deployed project from the Layer 2 session
- any session metadata allowed by protocol:
  - date
  - artifact type
  - whether participant returned
  - discipline/background
- interview form with time prompts

### During the Interview
- Open the participant's artifact before recording or during the first minute
- Ask the participant to describe what they see and what they remember doing
- Use the artifact as a memory anchor
- Do not correct the participant's account unless clarification is necessary
- Probe for process, not only outcome

## Client Interview Protocol With Probes

### Recommended Timing
- `0:00-2:00` consent and setup
- `2:00-5:00` artifact re-orientation
- `5:00-15:00` core questions
- `15:00-19:00` transfer, surprises, boundary cases
- `19:00-20:00` close

---

### Opening and Artifact Re-Orientation
1. "I'm going to pull up the artifact or project from your LearnAI session. As you look at it, can you tell me what this was and what you remember trying to do?"

Probes:
- "What was the original goal?"
- "What part of this feels most familiar as you look at it now?"
- "What do you remember being easy or hard at the start?"

2. "Can you walk me through what happened during the session, in your own words, from the beginning to the end?"

Probes:
- "What happened first?"
- "What did you and the tutor spend time figuring out before asking AI for anything?"
- "Where did AI become useful, and where was it not enough by itself?"

---

### Mental Model of AI: Oracle to Process Partner
3. "Before this session, how did you usually think about AI or what it was good for?"

Probes:
- "Did you see it mostly as a place to get answers, generate text, or something else?"
- "How much did you expect to direct the process yourself?"
- "What did you think your role was versus AI's role?"

4. "During or after the session, did the way you think about AI change at all?"

Probes:
- "Did it start to feel more like a tool, a collaborator, a search engine, a tutor, or something else?"
- "Did you still expect AI to give the final answer, or did you start using it differently?"
- "Can you give an example of a moment when your view changed?"

5. "If you had to explain AI to someone else after this session, what would you say its role is?"

Probes:
- "Would you describe it as an answer machine, a brainstorming partner, a drafting tool, a debugging partner, or something else?"
- "What part still requires the human?"

---

### LearnAI Process and the 5-Stage Script
6. "Looking back, what stands out about how the tutor guided the process?"

Probes:
- "Did they help you clarify the problem before using AI?"
- "Did they help choose the right tool for the task?"
- "Did they work with you on revising prompts?"
- "Did they push you to test, verify, or deploy something?"
- "Did ethical or responsible use come up?"

7. "Was there a point where you realized the session was not just about getting an output, but about learning a process?"

Probes:
- "What happened at that point?"
- "Was it tied to prompting, debugging, verification, deployment, or something else?"
- "Did the tutor make you slow down or rethink the task?"

---

### Surprise, Learning, and Transfer
8. "What surprised you most during the session?"

Probes:
- "Something AI could do?"
- "Something AI could not do?"
- "Something about how much direction it needed?"
- "Something about your own role in making it work?"

9. "What was the most useful thing you learned from the session?"

Probes:
- "A way of thinking?"
- "A practical workflow?"
- "A specific strategy for prompting, choosing tools, or checking outputs?"
- "Something you now do differently?"

10. "After the session, did you continue using AI on your own in any way?"

Probes:
- "Did you reuse the same process on a new task?"
- "Did you return to the artifact and extend it?"
- "Did you use AI more independently afterward?"
- "Did you avoid AI after the session? Why?"

11. "Can you tell me about a time after the session when you applied, adapted, or chose not to apply what you learned?"

Probes:
- "What was the task?"
- "Which parts transferred?"
- "What did not transfer?"
- "Did you need help again, or were you able to continue on your own?"

---

### Boundary Cases, Struggle, and Rejection
12. "Was there any part of the process that was frustrating, confusing, or not useful for you?"

Probes:
- "Problem framing?"
- "Choosing tools?"
- "Writing or revising prompts?"
- "Trusting or verifying AI output?"
- "Actually deploying or using the result?"
- "Ethical or privacy concerns?"

13. "Were there moments when you felt AI was not the right fit for your task?"

Probes:
- "What made it a bad fit?"
- "Did you stop using it, narrow the scope, or use it differently?"
- "Did the session increase your confidence, your caution, or both?"

14. "Some people decide AI is not worth using for certain tasks. Where do you draw that line now?"

Probes:
- "Tasks where you would use AI"
- "Tasks where you would not"
- "What makes the difference?"

---

### Closing Reflection
15. "If we wanted to improve LearnAI for future participants, what should stay the same and what should change?"

Probes:
- "What should tutors keep doing?"
- "What should be explained earlier?"
- "What would help someone become more independent afterward?"

16. "Is there anything important about your experience with LearnAI or AI more broadly that we did not ask about?"

## Client Thematic Coding Framework

### Coding Strategy
Use a hybrid codebook:
- `deductive` codes aligned to LearnAI constructs
- `inductive` subcodes for unexpected client experiences

A single segment may receive multiple codes.

### Primary Codes

| Code | Definition | Include When | Exclude When |
|---|---|---|---|
| `MM-Oracle` | Participant describes AI as answer machine, generator, authority, shortcut, or substitute for their own thinking | "I wanted it to just do it for me" | General positive comments without a model of AI |
| `MM-Partner` | Participant describes AI as collaborator, iterative tool, process support, thought partner, or draft/debug aid under human direction | "I had to steer it" | Mere use of AI without evidence of active orchestration |
| `MM-Shift` | Explicit comparison showing movement from oracle to partner model | Before/after contrast | Stable model with no change |
| `S1-ProblemFraming` | Evidence that defining scope, constraints, audience, or requirements mattered | Discussion of clarifying task before prompting | Generic planning not linked to AI use |
| `S2-ToolTaskMapping` | Evidence of choosing or rejecting tools based on task fit, constraints, access, privacy, or environment | "This tool was better because..." | General opinions about a tool with no task fit reasoning |
| `S3-CoPrompting` | Evidence of iterative prompting, refinement, decomposition, or back-and-forth prompt improvement | Revising prompts or outputs | One-shot prompting with no iteration |
| `S4-DeployVerify` | Evidence of testing, debugging, checking hallucinations, validating outputs, or moving into real use | Verification, deployment, error checking | Generic satisfaction with an output |
| `S5-Ethics` | Ethical reflection on privacy, bias, authorship, overreliance, policy, or appropriateness | Concerns or reasoning about responsible use | Purely technical errors |
| `SUR-Surprise` | Reported surprise about AI capability, limitation, or the process | "I didn't expect..." | Routine reflection without surprise |
| `TR-Transfer` | Applying or adapting the process to a new task independently | Reuse in later tasks | Return to same artifact without broader transfer |
| `IND-Independence` | Increased self-direction, confidence, or ability to continue without tutor support | "Now I can start on my own" | Confidence without behavioral evidence |
| `BC-Struggle` | Friction, confusion, failure, stalled progress, or mismatch between participant and process | Difficulty with any stage | Minor inconvenience without consequence |
| `BC-Rejection` | Deliberate refusal, discontinuation, or bounded refusal to use AI | "I decided not to use it for that" | Temporary frustration followed by continued use |
| `REL-Returner` | Reasons for coming back for another session or seeking additional support | Return behavior tied to value or limits | Generic praise |

### Recommended Secondary Subcodes
- `MM-AuthorityTrust`
- `MM-HumanControl`
- `S1-ConstraintArticulation`
- `S2-ContextualConstraint`
- `S3-IterationDiscipline`
- `S4-HallucinationDetection`
- `S4-DeploymentBarrier`
- `S5-PrivacyConcern`
- `TR-NewDomainTransfer`
- `BC-TechnicalBarrier`
- `BC-ConfidenceBarrier`
- `BC-ValueMisfit`

### Analytic Memos to Write During Coding
- What evidence supports or weakens the oracle-to-partner claim?
- Which stage appears most transformative for non-coders?
- Which stage breaks down most often in struggle cases?
- Do returners return because the framework works, because they need more support, or both?
- Are there client types for whom the LearnAI process does not fit well?

# Protocol 2: Tutor Interviews

## Purpose
To document how tutors actually enact the 5-Stage Pedagogical Script, how they adapt it across client types, where sessions break down, and what evidence supports intervention fidelity and replicability beyond individual tutor charisma.

## Target Sample
- `All 4 tutors`
- `~15 minutes` each

## Design Notes
These interviews should generate evidence that:
- the intervention has recognizable core practices
- tutors adapt within a shared structure rather than improvising idiosyncratically
- failures and deviations are visible and analyzable
- the framework could be trained and replicated by future tutors

## Interviewer Assignment Recommendation
If possible:
- avoid having a direct supervisor conduct the interview
- avoid pairing close peers if social desirability is likely
- use the same interviewer for all tutors if feasible for consistency

---

## Tutor Consent Script

"Thank you for meeting with me. We are conducting a research study on how LearnAI's Layer 2 sessions are carried out in practice. This interview focuses on how you guide clients through sessions, how you adapt the LearnAI process, and what patterns or challenges you have observed.

This interview should take about 15 minutes. Your participation is voluntary. You may skip any question or stop at any time without penalty. Participation or non-participation will not affect your employment, standing on the team, or relationship with the university.

With your permission, I would like to audio-record the interview so we can transcribe it accurately. We will remove names and direct identifiers from the transcript. De-identified quotations may be used in publications or presentations.

Do you have any questions?
Do I have your permission to proceed?
Do I have your permission to audio-record this interview?"

Record responses:
- Consent to participate: `Yes / No`
- Consent to record: `Yes / No`

---

## Tutor Interview Protocol With Probes

### Recommended Timing
- `0:00-2:00` consent
- `2:00-5:00` overall approach
- `5:00-11:00` script enactment and adaptation
- `11:00-14:00` failures, training, replicability
- `14:00-15:00` close

---

### Opening
1. "When you think about a typical LearnAI Layer 2 session, how do you usually approach it from start to finish?"

Probes:
- "What do you try to establish first?"
- "What parts feel most essential?"
- "What tends to vary by client?"

2. "How would you describe the LearnAI method to a new tutor in one or two minutes?"

Probes:
- "What are the non-negotiables?"
- "What would you tell them not to skip?"

---

### Enactment of the 5-Stage Script
3. "Let's go stage by stage. In practice, how do you help clients with problem framing?"

Probes:
- "How do you get from a vague idea to a workable task?"
- "What signs tell you the client is not yet ready to prompt?"
- "What mistakes do new tutors make here?"

4. "How do you handle tool-task mapping in real sessions?"

Probes:
- "How do you choose between tools?"
- "How do constraints shape that choice?"
- "How often do you steer a client away from the first tool they mention?"

5. "What does iterative co-prompting look like when it goes well?"

Probes:
- "What are you teaching the client to notice?"
- "How much do you model versus let them drive?"
- "What distinguishes a productive iteration from random trial and error?"

6. "How do deployment and verification show up in your sessions?"

Probes:
- "What counts as verification in practice?"
- "How do you handle hallucinations, brittle outputs, or false confidence?"
- "How often do clients want to stop at 'it looks good'?"

7. "When and how does ethical reflection come up?"

Probes:
- "Is it a separate stage or embedded throughout?"
- "What issues come up most often?"
- "Which clients are most or least receptive to this part?"

---

### Adaptation, Failure, and Boundary Cases
8. "How do you adapt the script for different kinds of clients?"

Probes:
- "Non-coders versus CS-adjacent participants?"
- "Staff versus students?"
- "Highly motivated returners versus skeptical participants?"
- "What changes and what stays constant?"

9. "Tell me about a session that did not go as planned."

Probes:
- "What went wrong?"
- "At what stage did it break down?"
- "Was the issue client expectations, tool limitations, scope, tutor move, or something else?"
- "What did you learn from that session?"

10. "Have you seen clients reject AI, resist the process, or fail to transfer what happened in the session?"

Probes:
- "What patterns do you notice in those cases?"
- "Are there warning signs early in the session?"
- "What does unsuccessful transfer look like?"

---

### Fidelity and Replicability
11. "In your view, what makes LearnAI work when it works?"

Probes:
- "Is it the tutor's personality, the script, the artifact focus, the one-on-one format, or some combination?"
- "Which elements are essential enough that another team would need to preserve them?"

12. "How consistent do you think tutor practice is across the team?"

Probes:
- "Where are tutors aligned?"
- "Where do they diverge?"
- "Are those differences healthy adaptation or a fidelity risk?"

13. "If another university wanted to replicate LearnAI, what would they need to train tutors to do well?"

Probes:
- "What skills are easiest to teach?"
- "What skills are hardest to teach?"
- "What examples, checklists, or rehearsal would help?"

14. "What training or support do you wish you had before doing this work?"

Probes:
- "Handling difficult sessions?"
- "Working with non-technical clients?"
- "Verification and deployment?"
- "Ethics conversations?"
- "Boundary setting around what AI should or should not do?"

---

### Closing
15. "What important pattern across clients or sessions do you think researchers might miss if they only looked at artifacts or survey scores?"

16. "Is there anything else you would want included in a paper about LearnAI so that readers understand how the tutoring actually works?"

## Tutor Thematic Coding Framework

### Primary Codes

| Code | Definition | Include When | Exclude When |
|---|---|---|---|
| `FID-Core` | Tutor identifies stable, non-negotiable elements of the LearnAI method | "We always start by clarifying the problem" | Vague descriptions of good teaching |
| `FID-Adapt` | Tutor describes variation around a stable core based on client needs | Adaptation by discipline, skill, or context | Idiosyncratic practice with no shared core |
| `SCR-S1` | Enactment of Problem Framing | Scope, constraints, audience, task decomposition | General coaching with no stage tie |
| `SCR-S2` | Enactment of Tool-Task Mapping | Choosing tools based on fit and constraints | Tool preference without decision rationale |
| `SCR-S3` | Enactment of Iterative Co-Prompting | Revising prompts, prompting discipline, client co-construction | One-shot tool use |
| `SCR-S4` | Enactment of Deployment & Verification | Testing, debugging, validation, deployment, trust-but-verify | Generic completion of a session |
| `SCR-S5` | Enactment of Ethical Reflection | Privacy, bias, ownership, policy, overreliance, appropriateness | Purely technical evaluation |
| `PAT-ClientPattern` | Cross-client patterns in uptake, struggle, transfer, or resistance | Repeated observations across cases | Single anecdote unless linked to broader pattern |
| `NEG-Breakdown` | Session failure, stall, derailment, or misfire | "This session went off the rails because..." | Minor inconvenience |
| `NEG-ExpectationMismatch` | Client expects AI to act as oracle or produce finished answer immediately | Unrealistic expectations | General confusion not tied to expectations |
| `NEG-TransferFailure` | Client does not continue independently or cannot generalize process | Lack of transfer after session | Need for more time within same task |
| `REP-Trainable` | Tutor claims methods can be taught, scaffolded, or documented | Replicability evidence | Praise of team without trainability claim |
| `REP-CharismaRisk` | Concern that outcomes depend too much on exceptional tutors or interpersonal skill | Risks to scaling | Normal adaptation |
| `TRN-Needed` | Training wishes or support needs | Missing onboarding, examples, scripts, role-play | General complaints unrelated to tutor preparation |

Map both protocols to the same analytic structure:

- `Mental model of AI`
  - oracle
  - mixed
  - process partner
- `Engagement with the 5-Stage Script`
  - stage-level evidence
  - missing stages
  - perceived most important stage
- `Transfer and independence`
  - independent reuse
  - assisted continuation
  - no continuation
- `Boundary conditions`
  - skepticism
  - overload
  - tool mismatch
  - discipline mismatch
  - ethical limits
- `Intervention fidelity and replicability`
  - stable core

- participant type

# Logistics and Timeline Recommendations

## Recommended Timeline
### Week 1
- finalize protocol language
- prepare IRB amendment or submission
- identify eligible client pool
- build purposive sample matrix
- assign study IDs

### Week 2
- recruit clients
- schedule tutor interviews
- run 1 pilot client interview and 1 pilot tutor interview
- refine probes for timing and clarity

### Weeks 3-4
- conduct all client interviews
- conduct all tutor interviews
- write interviewer field notes immediately after each session

### Weeks 4-5
- transcribe and de-identify
- begin coding with 2 coders on a shared subset
- revise code definitions after first calibration round

### Weeks 5-6
- code full dataset
- write analytic memos
- build mixed-methods joint displays
- identify illustrative positive and negative cases

## Logistics Recommendations
- Schedule client interviews within `1-6 weeks` of the Layer 2 session if possible to preserve recall while allowing transfer to occur.
- For returners, ask primarily about the first session, then probe later continuation.
- Use the actual artifact, not a generic description, for stimulated recall.
- Reserve `30 minutes` per client slot even though the interview target is 20 minutes.
- Reserve `20 minutes` per tutor slot even though the interview target is 15 minutes.
- Use a shared interviewer guide with scripted probes and timing prompts.
- Debrief interviewers after the first two interviews to identify leading questions or drift.
- Track recruitment to ensure negative and mixed cases are not crowded out by enthusiastic participants.

## Data Quality Safeguards
- Do not interview only highly satisfied or easy-to-contact clients.
- Actively recruit at least one participant who did not continue with AI.
- Separate service provision from recruitment where possible.
- Avoid framing the interview as proof that LearnAI "worked."
- Ask directly about confusion, rejection, and limits.

# Appendix C: Suggested Reporting Language for Methods Section

"Qualitative data were collected through two semi-structured interview protocols. First, we conducted stimulated recall interviews with 8-10 Layer 2 clients, using each participant's deployed artifact or project as a recall anchor. These interviews examined participants' mental models of AI, perceived learning, transfer to later tasks, and boundary cases including struggle or rejection. Second, we interviewed all four LearnAI tutors about enactment of the 5-Stage Pedagogical Script, adaptation across client types, session breakdowns, and tutor training needs. Interviews were audio-recorded with consent, transcribed, de-identified, and analyzed using hybrid thematic analysis combining deductive codes derived from the LearnAI Framework with inductive subcodes developed during analysis."
